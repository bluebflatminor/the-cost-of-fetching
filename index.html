Doing the math is nearly free.
Going to get the numbers is what costs.

How much energy a computer spends on five everyday jobs — all drawn at the same scale.

Drag the scale and the four cheap jobs swell up out of nothing. You're not discovering them — you're distorting the chart until they show. That distortion is the whole story.

Adding and multiplying numbers costs almost nothing. Nearly all the energy goes into fetching a number from far away and carrying it to where the math happens.

Put it as a walk: if adding two numbers is one footstep, then grabbing one number from the computer's main memory is walking about 700 steps — half a kilometre — to fetch it, every time.

That trip — not the thinking — is why computers, and especially AI, burn so much power.

Energy per operation, 45 nm chips — the standard reference figures from Horowitz (ISSCC 2014) and Dally. The money tag is an anchor for intuition (one decimal add ≈ 1¢), not a literal price.
